\begin{abstract}
We frame multilingual Discord content moderation as a Constrained Markov Decision
Process (CMDP). The agent learns a graduated action policy \textsc{Allow},
\textsc{Warn}, \textsc{Delete}, \textsc{Timeout}, \textsc{Ban} across 13
languages, with hard action masking enforcing procedural escalation (timeouts
require prior infractions, bans require prior timeouts). The 403-dimensional
observation combines multilingual sentence embeddings, per-language calibrated
XLM-R toxicity scores, user infraction history with cool-down decay, server heat
signals, and a language identity encoding. Fairness is enforced via an adaptive
Lagrangian multiplier penalizing false positives and a Disparate Impact Wrapper
that penalizes any language's ban rate exceeding $1.5\times$ the baseline.
Training is a two-stage behavior-cloning to MaskablePPO pipeline. Trained on
595 synthetic episodes (8,016 steps), the agent achieves 91.7\% accuracy on
\textsc{Delete}/\textsc{Timeout}/\textsc{Ban} categories where static
baselines score 0--4.5\% with false-negative rate below $10^{-4}$. Per-language
parity holds for 12 of 14 categories; residual Hindi/Hinglish disparate impact
is traced to upstream XLM-R bias.
\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}

Online communities face an escalating challenge in managing toxic behavior across
linguistically diverse user bases. Automated moderation systems traditionally
rely on keyword filters or static toxicity thresholds, which produce coarse
binary decisions (remove or permit) and fail to capture the graduated nature of
community enforcement~[1]. A single ban applied without prior warning violates
procedural norms users expect of moderation systems; conversely, allowing
repeated borderline behavior because no single message clears a fixed threshold
enables sustained harassment campaigns~[2].

Reinforcement learning offers a different framing: an agent that learns when to
warn, delete, temporarily suspend, and permanently ban based on the full
trajectory of a user's behavior and the toxicity of each message. Prior RL
approaches have been largely English-centric~[3] and have not addressed the
fairness problem that arises when classifier scores are systematically biased
across languages~[4].

We take this alternative seriously and frame moderation as a Constrained Markov
Decision Process. A single MaskablePPO policy operates over a five-level
escalation ladder (\textsc{Allow}, \textsc{Warn}, \textsc{Delete},
\textsc{Timeout}, \textsc{Ban}) and is shared across 13 languages, removing the
need for per-language models and making cross-language fairness comparisons
meaningful. Two design decisions distinguish the approach. First, the procedural
escalation rules---bans require prior timeouts, timeouts require prior
infractions---are enforced as hard action masks rather than reward penalties,
so the policy cannot even propose an illegal action. Second, fairness is treated
as a training-time objective rather than a post-hoc audit: an adaptive
Lagrangian multiplier suppresses false positives while a Disparate Impact
Wrapper taxes any language whose ban rate runs above $1.5\times$ the baseline.

The systematic per-language bias of the underlying XLM-R toxicity classifier is
neutralized by an affine recalibration applied before scores reach the policy,
and a language-aware regex layer overrides under-detected explicit threats so
\textsc{Ban} remains available when the classifier is unreliable. Training
proceeds in two stages---behavior cloning from an oracle escalation rule,
followed by MaskablePPO fine-tuning---with an escalation-consistency reward
shaping trajectory quality beyond per-step accuracy. On a held-out test split,
the agent is the only system tested that reaches \textsc{Timeout} and
\textsc{Ban} correctly, and a residual fairness audit isolates a
Hindi/Hinglish ban-rate gap and attributes it to upstream classifier bias
rather than policy bias. Source code:
\url{https://gitlab.cs.nuim.ie/2026/cs637/proj/fairmod}.


\section{Graduated moderation as a constrained MDP}
\label{sec:formulation}

We model moderation as a CMDP $\mathcal{M} = (S, A, P, R, \mathbf{C}, \mathbf{d}, \gamma)$
with observation space $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}^{403}$, discrete action space
$A = \{0,1,2,3,4\}$ corresponding to \{\textsc{Allow}, \textsc{Warn},
\textsc{Delete}, \textsc{Timeout}, \textsc{Ban}\}, transition kernel $P$, reward
$R$, a vector $\mathbf{C}$ of $K$ constraint cost functions with thresholds
$\mathbf{d}$, and discount factor $\gamma = 0.95$. The objective is to maximize
the return $V^\pi_R = \mathbb{E}_\pi[\sum_t \gamma^t R_t]$ subject to
$V^\pi_{C_k} \leq d_k$ for $k = 1, \ldots, K$. The constraints encode fairness
properties (false-positive rate; per-language ban-rate parity) that we want the
trained policy to respect, not merely optimize against on average.

We solve the CMDP via Lagrangian relaxation~[5,~14]. The primal--dual objective
$\mathcal{L}(\pi, \boldsymbol{\lambda}) = V^\pi_R - \sum_k \lambda_k (V^\pi_{C_k} - d_k)$
reduces to a standard MDP with augmented per-step reward
$\tilde{R}_t = R_t - \sum_k \lambda_k C_{k,t}$. The dual variable is updated by
projected gradient ascent on the constraint violation,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lambda_update}
  \lambda_{k+1} = \max\!\left(\epsilon,\;
    \lambda_k + \alpha_\lambda (\hat{V}_{C_k} - d_k)\right),
\end{equation}
with $\hat{V}_{C_k}$ estimated over a 1{,}000-step sliding window,
$\alpha_\lambda = 0.01$, and $\epsilon = 0.01$ preventing $\lambda_k$ collapse.

\paragraph{Reward.}
The base reward $R^{\text{base}}_t$ depends on the distance between the chosen
action $a_t$ and a ground-truth best action $a^*_t$ derived from message toxicity
and user history,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:base_reward}
  R^{\text{base}}_t =
  \begin{cases}
    +1.5 & a_t = a^*_t \\
    -0.5 & |a_t - a^*_t| = 1 \\
    -|a_t - a^*_t| & \text{otherwise.}
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
Context-sensitive penalties augment the base term: $-3.0$ for \textsc{Ban}
without a prior timeout, $-2.0$ for \textsc{Timeout} with $e_t < 2$, $-2.0$ for
any punitive action on benign content ($s_t < 0.20$), and $-3.0$ for
\textsc{Allow} on clearly toxic content ($s_t \geq 0.70$). The total is scaled
and clipped to $[-1,1]$, stabilizing the PPO value network~[6].


\section{Hard procedural enforcement via action masking}
\label{sec:masking}

A central design decision is to enforce the procedural escalation ladder at the
action-space level rather than through reward shaping alone. This is a stronger
guarantee: a learned policy cannot, even by accident, propose an illegal action
because masked actions are removed from the policy's distribution before
sampling. We implement this with a binary mask $\mathbf{m}_t \in \{0,1\}^5$ where
$m_{t,a} = 0$ renders action $a$ illegal at step $t$:
\begin{align}
  m_{t,4} &= 0 \;\text{if } h_t[\text{timeouts}] = 0 \label{eq:mask_ban}\\
  m_{t,3} &= 0 \;\text{if } e_t < 2 \label{eq:mask_timeout}\\
  m_{t,a} &= 0 \;\text{if } s_t < 0.15,\; a \in \{1,2,3,4\} \label{eq:mask_low_tox}\\
  m_{t,0} &= 0 \;\text{if } s_t \geq 0.85 \label{eq:mask_high_tox}
\end{align}
Here $s_t$ is the calibrated toxicity score and $e_t$ the effective infraction
count. \textsc{Ban} requires a prior timeout; \textsc{Timeout} requires at least
two prior infractions; punitive actions are forbidden on near-zero toxicity; and
\textsc{Allow} is forbidden on clearly toxic content. Table~\ref{tab:actions}
summarizes the action space.

\begin{table}[t]
  \caption{Action space and masking preconditions.}
  \label{tab:actions}
  \centering
  \begin{tabular}{cll}
    \toprule
    ID & Action & Precondition (must hold to be legal) \\
    \midrule
    0 & \textsc{Allow}   & $s_t < 0.85$ \\
    1 & \textsc{Warn}    & $s_t \geq 0.15$ \\
    2 & \textsc{Delete}  & $s_t \geq 0.15$ \\
    3 & \textsc{Timeout} & $s_t \geq 0.15$,\; $e_t \geq 2$ \\
    4 & \textsc{Ban}     & $s_t \geq 0.15$,\; $h_t[\text{timeouts}] \geq 1$ \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}


\section{One multilingual policy across 13 languages}
\label{sec:architecture}

A single MaskablePPO policy is trained over an observation
$\mathbf{o}_t = [\mathbf{e}_t \,\|\, s_t \,\|\, \mathbf{h}_t \,\|\, \mathbf{q}_t \,\|\, \mathbf{l}_t]
\in \mathbb{R}^{403}$ that fuses signals from five sources
(Table~\ref{tab:obs_space}). Putting all 13 languages through a shared policy
rather than per-language models makes fairness constraints meaningful: the
Disparate Impact Wrapper (Section~\ref{sec:fairness}) compares ban rates
\emph{across} languages within the same policy, which would be incoherent if each
language had its own model.

    $\mathbf{e}_t$ & Message embedding   & 384 & MiniLM-L12-v2~[13] \\
    $s_t$          & Toxicity score      & 1   & Calibrated XLM-R score $\in[0,1]$ \\
    $\mathbf{h}_t$ & User history        & 3   & Warns, timeouts, effective infractions \\
    $\mathbf{q}_t$ & Server context      & 2   & Rolling toxicity rate, action rate \\
    $\mathbf{l}_t$ & Language identity   & 13  & One-hot over 13 language codes \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}

\section{Per-language calibration of toxicity scores}
\label{sec:calibration}

Raw XLM-R toxicity scores are not comparable across languages: Arabic averages
$0.139$ below English-equivalent content; Italian, Ukrainian, and German are
also under-scored, while Hinglish is over-scored by $+0.095$ on average relative
to English. Feeding such scores directly into a shared policy bakes the
classifier's per-language bias into every downstream decision. We address this
with a per-language affine recalibration. For each language $l$, let
$s^l_{\text{low}}$ and $s^l_{\text{high}}$ be the 75th percentiles of benign and
toxic message scores in the training corpus; English anchors serve as the
calibration target. The calibrated score is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:calibration}
  \hat{s}(x, l) = \operatorname{clip}(s_{\text{raw}}(x) \cdot \sigma_l + \beta_l,\; 0,\; 1),
\end{equation}
with $\sigma_l = (s^{\text{en}}_{\text{high}} - s^{\text{en}}_{\text{low}}) /
(s^l_{\text{high}} - s^l_{\text{low}})$ and
$\beta_l = s^{\text{en}}_{\text{low}} - s^l_{\text{low}} \cdot \sigma_l$.
Calibration parameters are computed once and applied identically during training
and inference, so no train/test distribution shift is introduced.


\section{Dual fairness protection}
\label{sec:fairness}

We treat fairness as part of the optimization objective via two complementary
mechanisms. The \textbf{LagrangianPenaltyWrapper} implements
(\ref{eq:lambda_update}) with $\lambda_{\text{init}} = 0.3$,
$\alpha_\lambda = 0.01$, and false-positive threshold $d = 0.05$ over a
1{,}000-step window; any punitive action with $s_t < 0.30$ counts as a false
positive. The \textbf{DisparateImpactWrapper} monitors per-language \textsc{Ban}
rates over a rolling 2{,}000-step window and applies a $-2.0$ penalty whenever a
language $l$ satisfies $\hat{\rho}_l > 1.5 \cdot \hat{\rho}_{\text{all}}$ and the
current action is \textsc{Ban}. Together these turn fairness into a first-class
training signal rather than a post-hoc audit. The wrappers are applied in a
fixed order so that all penalty terms accumulate in raw reward space before the
final scaling step bounds the total in $[-1,1]$.


\section{Two-stage training and escalation-consistency reward}
\label{sec:training}

Recent work on scaling RL for content moderation~[15] reports that RL-only
training without a behavioral prior is unstable: the policy fails to converge
to a coherent reasoning schema. We therefore split training into two stages.
In \textbf{Stage~1 (behavior cloning)}, an oracle rule
$a^*_t = f(s_t, e_t, \tau_u)$ derived from the escalation ladder is rolled out
across all training episodes and the MaskablePPO policy is trained via NLL
minimization on the resulting $(\mathbf{o}_t, a^*_t, \mathbf{m}_t)$ triples
for 30 epochs (batch size 256, learning rate $3 \times 10^{-4}$). In
\textbf{Stage~2 (RL fine-tuning)}, the BC-initialized policy is loaded into
MaskablePPO~[7] from \texttt{stable-baselines3-contrib}~[8] and fine-tuned for
$10^6$ timesteps. We use $n_{\text{steps}} = 4096$, batch size $= 256$,
$n_{\text{epochs}} = 10$, $\gamma = 0.95$, learning rate $3 \times 10^{-4}$,
entropy coefficient $0.01$, max gradient norm $0.5$. Four parallel environments
yield an effective batch of $16{,}384$ transitions per update, well above the
$\sim$1{,}024 inflection point identified by~[15].

Beyond per-step accuracy, an \textit{escalation-consistency} component shapes
trajectory quality:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:esc_reward}
  R^{\text{esc}}_t =
  \begin{cases}
    +0.3 & a_t > a_{t-1},\; s_t - s_{t-1} > 0.10 \\
    +0.2 & a_t = 0,\; s_t < 0.20,\; c_t \geq 3 \\
    -0.4 & a_t - a_{t-1} \geq 2,\; s_t - s_{t-1} < 0.20 \\
    -0.3 & a_t > 0,\; c_t \geq 5,\; s_t < 0.30 \\
    0    & \text{otherwise.}
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
This rewards proportional escalation when toxicity rises, allows for
de-escalation when a user has been clean, and penalizes both unjustified
severity jumps and re-punishment of users who are visibly rehabilitating.
The combined reward is $R_t = R^{\text{base}}_t + R^{\text{esc}}_t$.


\section{Regex hard overrides for critical threats}
\label{sec:threats}

Neural classifiers under-detect explicit threats with surprising regularity, so
relying solely on $\hat{s}$ would leave a critical-harm gap. We add a
language-aware regex layer covering five categories (stalking, violence, death
wishes, swatting/doxxing, mass violence) across all 13 languages, using
character-based matching for Japanese and Chinese and right-to-left-safe
patterns for Arabic and Hebrew. When a pattern fires and $\hat{s} < 0.80$, the
score is overridden to $\max(\hat{s}, 0.85)$, ensuring \textsc{Ban} remains
available for explicit threats regardless of classifier confidence.
Death-threat patterns originally assumed verb-before-object word order; adding
SOV reverse-order variants for Russian, Ukrainian, Spanish, and Hindi raised
threat coverage from 9/13 to 13/13 languages.


\section{Synthetic data}

Training episodes are generated in three stages: (i) a balanced corpus of
$5{,}000$ toxic and $5{,}000$ benign examples per language is sourced from
Jigsaw~[11] and \texttt{textdetox/multilingual\_toxicity\_dataset}~[10];
(ii) $k$-means clustering on MiniLM embeddings yields diverse per-language
seeds; and (iii) DeepSeek~V3.1~[12] is prompted via Baseten to synthesize
multi-user Discord threads from those seeds. Thread types follow a fixed mix
(30\% toxic, 20\% escalating, 15\% mild, 10\% subtle, 25\% benign), with
Hinglish skewed to 20\%/30\% benign/mild. The final dataset comprises 595
episodes (8,016 steps) across 14 language categories
(Table~\ref{tab:lang_dist}) under an 80/20 train/test split.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\paragraph{Reaching higher-severity actions.}
Table~\ref{tab:baseline} compares the RL agent against two baselines on the
held-out test split. The \textbf{keyword filter} triggers \textsc{Delete} on
hand-crafted matches; all else \textsc{Allow}. The \textbf{static threshold}
maps toxicity ranges to fixed actions: $s < 0.15 \to$ \textsc{Allow},
$0.15 \leq s < 0.50 \to$ \textsc{Warn}, $s \geq 0.50 \to$ \textsc{Delete}.
Both baselines achieve $0\%$ on \textsc{Timeout} and \textsc{Ban}: they are
fundamentally unable to reach the higher-severity actions because they have no
notion of user history. The RL agent is the only system tested that produces
correct \textsc{Timeout} and \textsc{Ban} decisions, with per-class accuracies
of 91.6\% and 89.5\% (Table~\ref{tab:confusion}). Its false-negative rate is
$10^{-4}$, near zero. The elevated false-positive rate ($0.153$) reflects
conservative over-warning at the benign boundary; the majority of false
positives are \textsc{Warn} actions on benign content, a strictly lower-harm
error than false \textsc{Delete} or \textsc{Ban}.

\paragraph{Trajectory-level behavior.}
Three designed scenarios verify that the masking + escalation-consistency
combination produces the intended trajectory-level behavior. \textbf{Escalation:}
ten messages of monotonically increasing toxicity produce the full graduated
ladder \textsc{Allow}$^3 \to$ \textsc{Warn} $\to$ \textsc{Delete} $\to$
\textsc{Timeout}$^3 \to$ \textsc{Ban}, respecting all masking preconditions.
\textbf{Sustained troll:} a user alternating borderline-toxic and benign
messages without ever triggering a single high-severity event is correctly
escalated through recidivism (\textsc{Delete} $\to$ \textsc{Allow} $\to$
\textsc{Delete} $\to$ \textsc{Timeout}$^3$), confirming the policy uses
cumulative history rather than per-message severity.

\paragraph{Cross-lingual parity.}
Table~\ref{tab:parity} reports the XLM-R cross-lingual spread for six
representative sentence categories. Three categories show spreads $> 0.30$:
mild frustration (Arabic, Italian, Ukrainian, German under-detect), veiled
threats (Ukrainian, Italian near zero while Japanese near one), and severe
toxic content (Arabic $0.349$ vs.\ near-unity elsewhere). These reflect
fundamental limitations of the XLM-R pretrained weights rather than
calibration failures: affine calibration cannot supply signal that the
classifier does not produce.

\paragraph{Fairness audit and residual bias diagnosis.}
Table~\ref{tab:fairness} reports per-language \textsc{Ban} rates (overall
$0.066$). Twelve of fourteen categories pass the $1.5\times$ parity threshold;
Hindi ($1.92\times$) and Hinglish ($1.64\times$) exhibit residual disparate
impact. Cross-referencing with Table~\ref{tab:parity} attributes this
specifically to upstream classifier bias ($+0.095$ for Hindi vs.\ English) that
compounds through the escalation ladder. Importantly, aggressive calibration
offsets did not reduce the disparity, confirming the bias is structural to the
classifier rather than the policy. This separates policy bias from model bias
cleanly and points classifier fine-tuning~[16] as the recommended remediation.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented a CMDP framing of multilingual content moderation in which the
agent learns a graduated action policy across 13 languages, with action masking
providing hard procedural enforcement and a Lagrangian/Disparate Impact stack
making fairness a training-time objective. The two-stage BC$\to$RL pipeline
plus an escalation-consistency reward yields trajectory-level reasoning, and
language-aware regex overrides close the critical-harm gap left by classifier
under-detection. The agent is the only system tested that produces correct
higher-severity \textsc{Timeout} and \textsc{Ban} actions, with 89--94\%
per-class accuracy and a near-zero false-negative rate. The fairness audit
isolates residual Hindi/Hinglish disparate impact and attributes it to
upstream classifier bias rather than policy bias, separating concerns for
future work on classifier fine-tuning, disagreement-based reweighting~[15],
and multi-channel server graph extensions.
